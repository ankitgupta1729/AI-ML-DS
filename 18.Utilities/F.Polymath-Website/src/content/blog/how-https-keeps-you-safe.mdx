Every time you log in, pay a bill, or read your email in a browser, your data crosses dozens of machines you'll never see. HTTPS is the reason a café's Wi-Fi router, your ISP, and any eavesdropper in between can't read or tamper with it. It solves three problems at once: **secrecy**, **identity**, and **integrity**.

## Problem 1: agreeing on a secret in public

To encrypt traffic, both sides need a shared secret key — but they've never met. This is the chicken-and-egg of cryptography, and it's solved by **public-key** math (like [Diffie–Hellman](/blog/diffie-hellman-secret-in-public) or [elliptic curves](/blog/elliptic-curve-cryptography-intuition)): two strangers can exchange public values over an open line and *each* compute the same secret, while an observer who saw everything still can't.

## Problem 2: proving who you're talking to

A secret is useless if you accidentally set it up with an impostor. So servers present a **certificate** — a digital ID signed by a trusted **Certificate Authority (CA)**. Your browser ships with a list of CAs it trusts; it checks the signature to confirm the certificate for `yourbank.com` was really vouched for, and wasn't forged. This is what stops a "man in the middle" from impersonating the site.

## Problem 3: keeping it fast

Public-key crypto is powerful but slow. So it's used only briefly — for the handshake — to agree on a **symmetric key**. Everything afterward uses fast symmetric encryption (like AES), which is cheap enough to encrypt a video stream in real time.

## The handshake, start to finish

1. **Hello.** Your browser and the server agree on which crypto versions they'll use.
2. **Certificate.** The server sends its certificate; your browser verifies the CA's signature and that the name matches.
3. **Key exchange.** Using public-key math, both sides derive the *same* shared secret without ever sending it.
4. **Switch to symmetric.** From here on, all data is encrypted with that secret. Each message also carries an authentication tag, so any tampering is detected instantly.

All of this happens in a fraction of a second, before the page even loads.

## What the padlock does and doesn't promise

HTTPS guarantees the **connection** is private and the site is who its certificate says. It does **not** promise the site itself is honest — a scam site can also get a valid certificate. So the padlock means "no one is snooping on this conversation," not "trust this business." Knowing that distinction is half of staying safe online.
