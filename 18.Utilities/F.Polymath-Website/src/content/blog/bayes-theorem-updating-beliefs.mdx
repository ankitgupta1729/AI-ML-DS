Most people, including many smart ones, reason badly about evidence. They hear a scary test result and panic — often by a wildly wrong amount. Bayes' theorem is the cure: a precise rule for updating what you believe when you learn something new.

## The formula

```
P(A | B) = P(B | A) × P(A) / P(B)
```

- **P(A)** — the **prior**: your belief before the evidence.
- **P(A | B)** — the **posterior**: your updated belief after.
- **P(B | A)** — the **likelihood**: how expected the evidence is if A is true.

<Callout type="key">
With a 1% disease rate and a 99% test, a positive result means only about a **50%** chance of being sick — and if the disease is rarer (say 0.1%), it plummets to under 10%. The rarer the condition, the more false alarms swamp the true ones. This is the **base-rate fallacy**.
</Callout>

## Why this happens

Imagine 1,000 people. If 10 are sick, the test flags ~10 of them (true positives). But of the 990 healthy people, 1% — about 10 — get a false positive. So among ~20 positives, only half are truly sick. The disease is so rare that false alarms compete with real cases.

## Where Bayesian thinking shows up

- **Spam filters** start with a prior per word and update as your inbox teaches them.
- **Medical diagnosis** combines test results with how common a condition actually is.
- **Search and rescue** updates a probability map as each area is cleared.
- **Machine learning** — whole model families (Naive Bayes, Bayesian networks) are built on this rule.

Beyond the math, Bayes offers a mindset: **strong claims require strong evidence, and you should update in proportion.** Learn to think this way and you'll be wrong less often — which is, in the end, the whole point of statistics.
