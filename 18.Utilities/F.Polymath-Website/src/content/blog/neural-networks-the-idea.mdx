"Neural network" sounds intimidating — brains, intelligence, mystery. But the core idea is humble and beautiful: a network is just a big function with millions of adjustable knobs, and "learning" is the process of tuning those knobs until the function does something useful.

## From neuron to network

A single artificial neuron does three tiny things:

1. **Multiply** each input by a weight (its "importance").
2. **Add** them up, plus a bias.
3. **Squash** the result through a non-linear function (the "activation").

That's it. One neuron is almost trivial. The power comes from connecting thousands of them in layers, so the output of one layer becomes the input to the next.

## Why the "squash" matters

Without the non-linear activation, stacking layers would be pointless — a chain of straight lines is still just a straight line. The activation (like ReLU, which simply turns negatives to zero) lets the network bend and fold its input space, carving out the complex boundaries needed to recognize a cat or translate a sentence.

<Callout type="key">
A neural network is a **universal function approximator**: with enough neurons, it can approximate essentially any input-to-output mapping. Training is the search for the weights that capture the *right* one.
</Callout>

## How it learns

Learning has three beats, repeated millions of times:

1. **Forward pass** — feed in data, get a prediction.
2. **Measure error** — compare the prediction to the truth via a loss function.
3. **Backpropagation + [gradient descent](/blog/gradient-descent-intuition/)** — nudge every weight slightly in the direction that reduces the error.

Do this over a huge dataset and the network gradually configures itself. Nobody hand-codes the rules; the data writes them.

## Why depth helps

Early layers learn simple patterns (edges, sounds, word fragments). Later layers combine these into richer concepts (faces, phrases, ideas). This hierarchy of features — simple to abstract — is why "deep" learning is so powerful, and why it loosely echoes how our own perception is organized.
