We treat zero as obvious — it's right there on every keyboard. But for most of human history, there was no zero. Great civilizations did sophisticated mathematics, astronomy, and engineering entirely without it. The invention of zero was one of the hardest conceptual leaps our species ever made, and it changed the world.

## Two different zeros

It helps to separate two ideas that both got called "zero":

1. **Zero as a placeholder** — the symbol that distinguishes 25 from 205 from 250. Without it, written numbers are ambiguous.
2. **Zero as a number** — a quantity in its own right that you can add, subtract, and compute with. *Nothing*, treated as *something*.

The first is useful bookkeeping. The second is revolutionary — and far more controversial.

## A long road

The Babylonians (around 300 BCE) used a placeholder symbol in their base-60 system, but never treated it as a true number. The Maya independently developed a zero for their calendars. But it was in **India** that zero came fully into its own. By the 7th century, the mathematician **Brahmagupta** wrote down rules for arithmetic with zero and negative numbers — treating zero as a genuine number you could operate on.

From India the idea traveled through the **Islamic world**, where scholars like al-Khwarizmi (whose name gives us "algorithm") spread the Hindu-Arabic numeral system. From there it reached Europe — slowly, and against fierce resistance.

## Why people fought it

Zero was genuinely unsettling. How can *nothing* be a *thing*? Medieval Europe was suspicious enough that some cities **banned** the new numerals, partly fearing they were easier to forge than Roman numerals. Philosophers worried zero smelled of the void, even the diabolical. It took centuries for "nothing" to become respectable.

And zero really does behave strangely. You can add and multiply with it happily — but divide by it and mathematics breaks. That wasn't ancient confusion; division by zero is genuinely undefined, a sign of how subtle the concept is.

## Why it mattered so much

Zero unlocked nearly everything that followed:

- **Place-value arithmetic.** Try multiplying CCXLVII by MDCCLXIX in Roman numerals. The Hindu-Arabic system, with zero, makes calculation trivial — fueling commerce, accounting, and science.
- **Algebra.** Brahmagupta's rules for zero and negatives made algebra possible.
- **Calculus.** Limits approaching zero are the heart of calculus, and thus of all modern physics and engineering.
- **Computing.** Binary — the 0s and 1s every computer runs on — quite literally cannot exist without zero.

## The takeaway

Zero is a reminder that the most powerful ideas are often the ones that seem like nothing at all. It took thousands of years for humanity to look at emptiness and see a number — and once we did, mathematics, science, and eventually computing became possible. Not bad for *nothing*.
