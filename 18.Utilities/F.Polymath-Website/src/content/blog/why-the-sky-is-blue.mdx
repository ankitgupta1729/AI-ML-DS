It's the question every curious child asks and most adults can't quite answer. The sky is blue because of a beautiful interplay between sunlight, the tiny molecules of our atmosphere, and a steep mathematical law. Let's settle it.

## Sunlight is secretly every color

Sunlight looks white, but it's a mix of all visible colors — each with a different **wavelength**. Red light has long, lazy waves; blue and violet light have short, tight waves. A prism (or a raindrop) spreads them into a rainbow precisely because they bend by different amounts.

## Enter Rayleigh scattering

As sunlight streams through the atmosphere, it bumps into nitrogen and oxygen molecules far smaller than its wavelength. These molecules **scatter** the light — and here's the crucial part: shorter wavelengths scatter *far* more strongly. The effect follows an inverse fourth-power law:

```
scattering ∝ 1 / wavelength⁴
```

That fourth power is dramatic. Blue light (~450 nm) scatters roughly **5–6 times more** than red light (~700 nm). So as sunlight crosses the sky, blue is sprayed in all directions while red mostly continues straight.

<Callout type="question" title="Wait — why not violet?">
Violet scatters even more than blue! But two things favor blue: the Sun emits a bit less violet, and our eyes are far more sensitive to blue than violet. The sky we *perceive* is the result of physics **and** human biology.
</Callout>

## Why sunsets are red

At sunset, light skims through much more atmosphere to reach you. Along that long path, almost all the blue has been scattered away before it arrives — leaving the reds and oranges to pass straight into your eyes. The same scattering that makes a noon sky blue makes an evening sky blaze red. One law, two gorgeous effects.

## Why clouds are white and space is black

- **Clouds** are made of water droplets *larger* than light's wavelength. These scatter all colors roughly equally (Mie scattering), so clouds look white or grey.
- **Space** has almost no molecules to scatter light, so the sky there is black even in direct sunlight — as every astronaut photo shows.
