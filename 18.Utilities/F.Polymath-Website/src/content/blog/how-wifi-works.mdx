Wi-Fi feels like sorcery: data appears out of thin air. But underneath, it's a beautifully engineered version of something a century old — radio. Your router broadcasts, your devices listen and reply, and your cat videos ride electromagnetic waves traveling at the speed of light.

## It's radio with a vocabulary

Wi-Fi sends information as **radio waves**, the same family as broadcast radio and your microwave oven — just at specific frequencies (commonly **2.4 GHz and 5 GHz**, with **6 GHz** in newer gear). To carry data, the wave is **modulated**: its properties are nudged in patterns that encode the 1s and 0s of your data, millions of times per second.

<Callout type="info" title="2.4 vs 5 vs 6 GHz">
**Lower frequency (2.4 GHz)** travels farther and through walls better, but is slower and more crowded (it shares space with Bluetooth, microwaves, baby monitors). **Higher frequencies (5/6 GHz)** are faster with less interference, but have shorter range. That's the eternal Wi-Fi trade-off: reach vs. speed.
</Callout>

## Packets and politeness

Your data isn't sent as one big stream. It's chopped into small **packets**, each labeled with where it's going. This is the same idea that runs [the whole internet](/blog/how-the-internet-actually-works/) — packets can be sent, lost, re-sent, and reassembled independently.

Because everyone on a network shares the airwaves, devices must take turns. Wi-Fi uses a "listen before you talk" rule: a device checks if the channel is free, and if two devices talk at once (a collision), they wait a random moment and retry. It's remarkably like polite conversation at a dinner party.

## From router to the world

Your router does two jobs:

1. **Wireless access point** — the radio that talks to your phone and laptop.
2. **Gateway** — the bridge between your home network and your internet provider (usually over a fiber or cable line).

So when you open a website, the request hops wirelessly to your router, then travels by wire across the globe and back — all in the blink of an eye.

## Why your Wi-Fi is slow (and how to fix it)

- **Distance and walls.** Signal weakens with every obstacle. Place the router central and high.
- **Interference.** Microwaves and neighbors' networks crowd 2.4 GHz. Switching to 5 GHz often helps.
- **Channel congestion.** Many networks on the same channel fight for airtime.
- **Old hardware.** Wi-Fi standards (Wi-Fi 5, 6, 6E, 7) keep getting faster and better at handling many devices at once.

<Callout type="tip">
A common myth: more "bars" means faster internet. Bars show *signal strength to your router*, not your internet speed. A strong signal to a slow connection is still slow.
</Callout>
