If you've used ChatGPT, Claude, or any modern AI assistant, you've used a **transformer**. And at the heart of every transformer is one deceptively simple idea: *attention*.

## The problem attention solves

Language is full of long-range dependencies. Consider:

> "The trophy didn't fit in the suitcase because **it** was too big."

What does "it" refer to — the trophy or the suitcase? You resolved this instantly using context. Older models (RNNs) processed words one at a time, squeezing all earlier context into a single fixed-size memory. By the time they reached "it," much nuance was gone. Attention throws out that bottleneck.

## The core idea: queries, keys, and values

Each word is turned into three vectors:

- **Query (Q):** "What am I looking for?"
- **Key (K):** "What do I offer?"
- **Value (V):** "What information do I carry?"

To compute attention for a word, we compare its query against every key. A high match means "pay attention here." Those scores become weights, and we take a weighted average of the values.

In one line of math:

```
Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax( QKᵀ / √dₖ ) V
```

The `softmax` turns raw scores into probabilities that sum to 1; the `√dₖ` keeps numbers stable. Everything else in a transformer is plumbing around this operation.

<Callout type="key" title="Why 'self' attention is special">
In self-attention, Q, K, and V all come from the same sentence — every word attends to every other word, including itself. So the representation of "it" can absorb meaning from "trophy" or "suitcase" depending on what the model learned matters.
</Callout>

## Multi-head attention: many perspectives at once

One attention calculation captures one kind of relationship. Real language has many at once: grammar, meaning, reference, tone. So transformers run several attention operations in parallel — **heads** — each free to specialize. Their outputs are concatenated and mixed.

## Why this design won

1. **Parallelism.** Every position is computed at once — perfect for GPUs.
2. **Direct long-range links.** Any word can reach any other in one step.
3. **Scalability.** Performance keeps improving with more data and parameters — the foundation of today's LLMs.
