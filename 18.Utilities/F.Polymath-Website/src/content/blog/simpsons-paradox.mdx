Here's a fact that breaks people's brains: a trend can appear in every single group of your data and then **reverse** when you combine the groups. It's called **Simpson's paradox**, and it's not a trick of bad data — it's a genuine feature of how averages work.

## A real example

The University of California, Berkeley was once accused of gender bias in graduate admissions: men were admitted at a noticeably higher rate than women, overall. But when researchers looked *department by department*, most departments actually favored women slightly. How can both be true?

The answer: women applied disproportionately to highly competitive departments with low acceptance rates, while men applied more to easier-to-enter ones. The *mix* of applications, not bias within departments, drove the overall gap.

## Why it happens

Simpson's paradox appears when a hidden **confounding variable** (here, *which department*) is unevenly distributed across the groups you're comparing. The overall average secretly mixes together groups of very different sizes and baselines, and that mixing can flip the apparent direction of an effect.

<Callout type="warning">
The lesson isn't "always split your data" — sometimes the aggregate is the honest number, and slicing too finely invents patterns. The real lesson is that **the right answer depends on the causal question you're asking**, and you must think about confounders before trusting *either* view.
</Callout>

## It has real consequences

- **Medicine:** a treatment can look worse overall if sicker patients were more likely to receive it — even if it helps everyone.
- **Hiring and pay:** aggregate gaps can hide or exaggerate what's happening role by role.
- **Sports and business metrics:** combining seasons, regions, or products can reverse a trend that's consistent within each.

This is a close cousin of [correlation vs. causation](/blog/correlation-is-not-causation/): the numbers are all correct; the *interpretation* is where things go wrong.

## How to protect yourself

1. **Ask what's being averaged.** Are the subgroups very different in size or baseline?
2. **Look for a lurking variable** that differs across groups.
3. **Decide the causal question first.** "Does the treatment help a patient?" and "What happens to the population on average?" can have different right answers.

Simpson's paradox is a humbling reminder that data never speaks for itself. The same honest numbers can tell opposite stories depending on how you slice them — and knowing which story is *true* requires thinking about cause, not just calculating averages.
