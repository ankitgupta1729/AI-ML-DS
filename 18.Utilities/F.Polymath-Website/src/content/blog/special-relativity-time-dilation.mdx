In 1905, a 26-year-old patent clerk named Albert Einstein published an idea that broke one of our deepest assumptions: that time ticks the same for everyone, everywhere. It doesn't — and this isn't philosophy. It's measured, confirmed, and quietly correcting your phone's GPS right now.

## Two postulates, one radical conclusion

1. **The laws of physics are the same for all observers moving at constant velocity.**
2. **The speed of light in a vacuum is the same for everyone**, regardless of their motion.

That second one is the troublemaker. Normally speeds add up. But light refuses: whether you race toward a beam or away from it, you always measure it at ~300,000 km/s. If light's speed is fixed for everyone, something else must give — and that something is **time and space themselves.**

## The light clock

Picture a photon bouncing between two mirrors — one tick per round trip. When this clock flies past you, the photon must travel a longer *diagonal* path. But light can't speed up to cover the extra distance, so each tick takes longer. The moving clock runs slow.

The effect grows with speed:

```
t_moving = t_rest / √(1 − v²/c²)
```

At walking pace it's unmeasurably tiny; near light-speed it becomes enormous.

<Callout type="key" title="The twin 'paradox'">
A twin who rockets away near light-speed and returns comes back **younger** — genuinely fewer seconds elapsed for them. It isn't symmetric because the traveler accelerated and turned around. Particle physicists see this daily: fast muons "live" far longer than stationary ones, exactly as predicted.
</Callout>

## Proof in your pocket

GPS satellites carry atomic clocks and move fast relative to the ground. Special relativity makes their clocks tick slower; general relativity (weaker gravity up high) makes them tick faster. The net effect is ~38 microseconds/day. Ignore it and GPS would drift by kilometers within hours. Engineers correct for Einstein's equations continuously — **relativity is infrastructure.**
