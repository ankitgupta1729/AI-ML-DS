Most students find rotational motion intimidating. The secret JEE toppers know: **every rotational quantity is just the linear one with a twist.** Learn the analogy once and half the formulas come for free.

## The master analogy

| Linear | Rotational | Link |
|---|---|---|
| mass $m$ | moment of inertia $I$ | how mass is *spread out* |
| force $F$ | torque $\tau = rF\sin\theta$ | what causes change |
| velocity $v$ | angular velocity $\omega$ | $v = r\omega$ |
| momentum $p = mv$ | angular momentum $L = I\omega$ | conserved when no external torque |
| $F = ma$ | $\tau = I\alpha$ | Newton's 2nd law |
| KE $= \tfrac12 mv^2$ | KE $= \tfrac12 I\omega^2$ | energy |

<Callout type="key">
Moment of inertia $I = \sum m_i r_i^2$ depends on the **axis** — the same body has different $I$ about different axes. Mass far from the axis counts much more (it's squared). That's why a figure skater spins faster when they pull their arms in: $I$ drops, and since $L = I\omega$ is conserved, $\omega$ shoots up.
</Callout>

## Torque: the rotational "push"

Torque measures how effectively a force *rotates* something:

$$\tau = r F \sin\theta$$

Push a door near the hinges (small $r$) and nothing happens; push at the handle, perpendicular ($\theta = 90°$), and it swings easily. JEE loves this: maximize torque ⇒ apply force far from the axis, perpendicular to the lever arm.

## Angular momentum conservation — the JEE favourite

When net external torque is zero, $L = I\omega$ stays constant. This single idea cracks a huge class of problems: collisions on turntables, the skater, a person walking on a rotating platform. **Spot "no external torque" and write $I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2$.**

## Rolling without slipping

A rolling body has both translation and rotation. The key constraint is $v = r\omega$, and its kinetic energy splits:

$$KE = \tfrac12 m v^2 + \tfrac12 I\omega^2$$

<Callout type="tip">
On an incline, a body's acceleration depends only on the shape factor $I/mr^2$ — **not** on its mass or radius. So a ring ($I = mr^2$) always loses the race to a solid sphere ($I = \tfrac25 mr^2$), no matter their sizes. Memorise the order: sphere > disc > ring.
</Callout>
