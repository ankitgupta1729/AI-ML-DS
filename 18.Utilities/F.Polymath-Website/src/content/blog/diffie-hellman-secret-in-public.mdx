Here's a puzzle that sounds impossible. Two people who have never met want to agree on a secret password. But every word they say is broadcast to the whole world. How can they end up sharing a secret that eavesdroppers *can't* figure out — even though those eavesdroppers heard every message? In 1976, Diffie and Hellman solved it, and it's one of the most elegant ideas in all of computing.

## The paint analogy

Forget the math for a moment. Imagine mixing paint:

1. Alice and Bob **publicly** agree on a common color, say yellow. Everyone sees it.
2. Each secretly picks a private color. Alice picks red; Bob picks blue. They tell no one.
3. Each mixes their secret into the public yellow and **sends the result**. Alice sends orange (yellow + red); Bob sends green (yellow + blue). Eavesdroppers see orange and green.
4. Now each adds their *own* secret to the color they received. Alice adds red to Bob's green; Bob adds blue to Alice's orange.

Both end up with the **same** muddy brown — yellow + red + blue. But an eavesdropper, holding only yellow, orange, and green, can't reproduce it, because **unmixing paint is hard**. They'd need a private color, and those were never sent.

## The real math

Diffie–Hellman swaps paint for **modular exponentiation**. Publicly agree on a base `g` and a large prime `p`. Alice picks a secret `a`, Bob a secret `b`.

- Alice sends `A = gᵃ mod p`.
- Bob sends `B = gᵇ mod p`.
- Alice computes `Bᵃ mod p`; Bob computes `Aᵇ mod p`.

Both equal `gᵃᵇ mod p` — the shared secret. An eavesdropper sees `g`, `p`, `A`, and `B`, but to find the secret they'd need `a` or `b`. Recovering those from `gᵃ mod p` is the **discrete logarithm problem** — easy to compute forward, infeasible to reverse. That's the "unmixing paint is hard" step, made rigorous.

<Callout type="key">
Diffie–Hellman lets two parties create a shared key over an open channel **without ever transmitting it**. It's why you can connect securely to a website you've never visited — the encryption key is born during the handshake, not sent across the wire.
</Callout>

## Where you use it

Every time you load an HTTPS page, a variant of this (usually the faster *elliptic-curve* Diffie–Hellman) runs in milliseconds to establish the session key. Modern setups even pick a fresh secret every session — **forward secrecy** — so that even if a key is later compromised, past conversations stay safe. It pairs naturally with [RSA](/blog/how-rsa-encryption-works/) and [hash functions](/blog/hash-functions-explained/) to secure the web.
