## Linear vs. exponential intuition

Ask someone to fold a piece of paper 42 times and guess its thickness. Most say "a few centimeters." The real answer: thick enough to reach the Moon. Each fold *doubles* the previous one. Our brains simply aren't wired for repeated multiplication — and that blind spot is exactly why compounding feels like magic.

## The formula, decoded

```
Future Value = P × (1 + r)ⁿ
```

- **P** — your principal (starting amount)
- **r** — return per period
- **n** — number of periods

The power lives in that exponent `n`. Returns earn returns, which themselves earn returns.

## Why starting early beats investing more

Consider two savers, both earning 8% annually:

- **Anita** invests \$5,000/year from age 25–35 (10 years, \$50,000 total), then stops forever.
- **Ben** invests \$5,000/year from age 35–65 (30 years, \$150,000 total).

At 65, despite investing **three times more money**, Ben ends up with *less* than Anita. Her early contributions had decades longer to compound. Try to reproduce this in the calculator — it's startling.

<Callout type="key" title="The Rule of 72">
Divide 72 by your annual return to estimate how long money takes to double. At 8%: 72 / 8 = **9 years**. At 4%: **18 years**. A great mental shortcut.
</Callout>

## Compounding cuts both ways

- **Debt.** Credit-card interest at 24% APR doubles what you owe in about three years if unpaid.
- **Fees.** A 2% annual fee can quietly consume a third or more of your final balance over 30 years — because that 2% would have compounded too.
- **Inflation.** At 3% inflation, prices double in about 24 years. Idle money silently loses purchasing power.

## The honest lesson

You don't need to be brilliant to build wealth. Start early, keep costs low, avoid high-interest debt, and let time do the heavy lifting. The hardest part isn't the math — it's the patience.
