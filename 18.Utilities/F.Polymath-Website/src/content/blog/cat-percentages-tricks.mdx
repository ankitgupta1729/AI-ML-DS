In CAT, *how fast* you handle percentages decides your quant score. The trick is to think in **fractions and multipliers**, not long division.

## Memorise the fraction ↔ percent table

| Fraction | % | Fraction | % |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1/2 | 50% | 1/6 | 16.67% |
| 1/3 | 33.33% | 1/7 | 14.28% |
| 1/4 | 25% | 1/8 | 12.5% |
| 1/5 | 20% | 1/9 | 11.11% |

So "62.5% of 80" = $\tfrac58 \times 80 = 50$ — instant, no calculator.

## The multiplier method

A change of $x\%$ multiplies by $\left(1 + \tfrac{x}{100}\right)$.

- 20% increase → ×1.2; 20% decrease → ×0.8.
- Successive 10% then 20% rise → ×1.1 × 1.2 = ×1.32 (a **32%** net rise, not 30%).

<Callout type="key">
A rise of $a\%$ then a fall of $a\%$ does **not** return to the start — it gives a net change of $-\tfrac{a^2}{100}\%$. E.g., +10% then −10% ⇒ −1%. CAT loves this trap.
</Callout>

## Percentage ↔ ratio

"A is 25% more than B" means $A = 1.25B = \tfrac54 B$, so $A:B = 5:4$. Converting between percentages and ratios instantly is half the battle in DI/LR sets too.

<Callout type="tip">
To find "B is what % less than A" when $A:B = 5:4$: difference is 1 part out of 5 ⇒ **20%**. Always divide the difference by the **reference** quantity (the one after "than").
</Callout>
