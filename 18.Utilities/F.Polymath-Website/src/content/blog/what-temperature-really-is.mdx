We talk about temperature as if it were a fluid — heat "flows in," cold "gets in through the window." Useful language, wrong picture. Temperature is really about **motion you can't see**.

## Heat is just jiggling

Every atom in a substance is in constant random motion — vibrating in a solid, zipping around in a gas. **Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of that motion.** Hotter means the particles are, on average, moving faster.

For an ideal gas this is exact: the average kinetic energy per particle is

$$\langle KE \rangle = \tfrac{3}{2} k_B T$$

where $T$ is the absolute temperature and $k_B$ is Boltzmann's constant — the conversion rate between "degrees" and "energy of jiggling."

## Why absolute zero is a floor

If temperature is average motion, then the coldest possible state is when motion is *minimal*. That point is **absolute zero** ($0\ \text{K}$, about $-273.15\,°\text{C}$). You can't go below it for the same reason you can't drive slower than stopped. This is why physicists measure temperature in **kelvin** — a scale that starts where the motion does.

## "Hot" and "cold" objects, one rule

Heat doesn't seek out cold; fast particles simply collide with slow ones and share energy until both sides have the same average. That one-way averaging — energy spreading from more-energetic to less-energetic until balance — is the whole content of the **second law of thermodynamics** at the everyday scale.

## The metal-feels-colder puzzle

Touch metal and wood in the same room. The metal feels colder — yet a thermometer says they're identical. The sensation isn't measuring temperature; it's measuring **how fast heat leaves your hand**. Metal conducts heat away quickly, so your skin cools fast and screams "cold." Wood is a poor conductor, so your hand stays warm. Your nerves report energy *flow*, not temperature.

## Why the reframing matters

Once temperature becomes "average molecular motion," a pile of separate facts collapses into one: gases expand when heated (faster particles push harder), evaporation cools (the fastest molecules escape, lowering the average left behind), and there's a hard cold limit but no hot one. The thermometer was never measuring a substance — it was counting jiggles.
