The mole is just a **counting unit**, like "a dozen" — only much bigger. One mole = $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ particles (Avogadro's number, $N_A$). Every stoichiometry problem is secretly a units-conversion problem with the mole as the hub.

## The three bridges

$$n = \frac{m}{M} = \frac{N}{N_A} = \frac{V}{22.4\ \text{L}}\ (\text{gas at STP})$$

- **mass ↔ moles:** $n = m/M$ (M = molar mass in g/mol)
- **particles ↔ moles:** $n = N/N_A$
- **gas volume ↔ moles:** at STP, 1 mol of any ideal gas = 22.4 L

<Callout type="key">
Almost every JEE stoichiometry question is: convert the given quantity **to moles**, use the balanced equation's mole ratio, then convert **back** to whatever is asked. Moles are the universal currency.
</Callout>

## Worked example

*How many grams of $O_2$ are needed to completely burn 16 g of $CH_4$?*

$$CH_4 + 2O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O$$

1. Moles of $CH_4 = 16/16 = 1$ mol.
2. Mole ratio $CH_4 : O_2 = 1 : 2$, so $O_2 = 2$ mol.
3. Mass of $O_2 = 2 \times 32 = 64$ g. ✓

## Limiting reagent — the classic trap

When two reactants are given, one runs out first (the **limiting reagent**) and decides the product. Compute moles of each, divide by their coefficients, and the **smallest** ratio limits the reaction.

<Callout type="warning">
Don't compare masses — compare **moles ÷ coefficient**. A heavier reactant can still be in excess. This is the single most common stoichiometry mistake in JEE.
</Callout>
