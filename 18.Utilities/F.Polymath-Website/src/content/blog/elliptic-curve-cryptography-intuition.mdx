Public-key cryptography needs a **one-way street**: an operation easy to do and miserable to undo. RSA uses multiplying primes (easy) versus factoring (hard). Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC) uses a different, more efficient trick — and it's more geometric than it sounds.

## The curve

An elliptic curve is just the set of points $(x, y)$ satisfying an equation like

$$y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$$

It's a smooth, symmetric curve. The clever part is that you can define a way to **"add" two points** on it.

## Adding points (the weird, wonderful rule)

To add points $P$ and $Q$: draw the line through them. It hits the curve at exactly one more point; reflect that across the x-axis, and *that's* $P + Q$. It looks arbitrary, but this operation behaves beautifully — it's associative, has an identity, and gives every point an inverse. Mathematicians call that a **group**.

The key move is adding a point to **itself, over and over**: $P, 2P, 3P, \dots, kP$. Computers can compute $kP$ for an enormous $k$ very fast (by doubling repeatedly).

## The one-way street

Here's the trap door. Given $P$ and the result $kP$, finding the multiplier $k$ is the **elliptic curve discrete logarithm problem** — and no one knows a fast way to do it. So:

- Your **private key** is the secret number $k$.
- Your **public key** is the point $kP$ (which you can share freely).

Anyone can verify things with $kP$; nobody can run the operation backward to recover $k$.

## Why everyone switched to it

The discrete-log problem on curves is *so* hard that ECC needs far smaller keys for the same security:

- A **256-bit** ECC key is roughly as strong as a **3072-bit** RSA key.

Smaller keys mean less data to send, less battery to spend, and faster handshakes — which is exactly what phones, smart cards, and the modern web need. When you open an `https://` site today, there's a good chance two points were bounced around a curve to set up your secure connection. ([Diffie–Hellman](/blog/diffie-hellman-secret-in-public) and [RSA](/blog/how-rsa-encryption-works) are the classic cousins of this idea.)
