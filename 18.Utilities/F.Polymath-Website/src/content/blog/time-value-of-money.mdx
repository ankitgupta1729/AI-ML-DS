Would you rather have ₹1000 now or ₹1000 a year from now? Obviously now — you could invest it, or inflation could erode the later amount. That gut feeling has a precise name: the **time value of money**. Almost all of finance is this one idea, applied carefully.

## Future value: growing forward

Money left to earn a rate $r$ grows by compounding. After $n$ years, an amount $P$ becomes:

$$FV = P\,(1 + r)^n$$

At 8% a year, ₹1000 becomes ₹1469 in five years — and the *growth of the growth* is where the magic is. The interest earns interest.

## Present value: discounting backward

Flip it around. If someone promises you ₹1000 in five years, what's that worth **today**? Divide by the same growth factor:

$$PV = \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^n}$$

At 8%, that future ₹1000 is worth only about ₹681 now. The further away a payment, the more we **discount** it. This is the engine behind valuing a bond, a business, or a stream of salary.

## Why it explains so much

- **Loans:** a bank lends today and is repaid more later — the extra is the price of time (plus risk).
- **Net Present Value (NPV):** to judge a project, discount every future cash flow back to today and add them up. Positive NPV means it beats the alternative of just earning $r$.
- **The Rule of 72:** money doubles in roughly $72 / r$ years. At 9%, about 8 years. A handy mental shortcut.

## The lesson hiding inside

The discount rate $r$ is really a statement about **opportunity** and **risk**. A higher $r$ crushes far-off payments — which is why risky, far-future promises are worth surprisingly little today, and why starting to invest *early* matters far more than investing *more* later. Time, compounded, does the heavy lifting.
