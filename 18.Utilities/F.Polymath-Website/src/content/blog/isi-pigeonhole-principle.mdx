The pigeonhole principle sounds almost too obvious to be useful: **if you put $n+1$ pigeons into $n$ holes, some hole has at least two pigeons.** Yet it powers some of the most elegant proofs in ISI/CMI and olympiad maths. The skill is *recognising* the pigeons and the holes.

## The statement

- **Simple form:** $n+1$ objects in $n$ boxes ⇒ some box has ≥ 2 objects.
- **Generalised form:** $N$ objects in $n$ boxes ⇒ some box has at least $\lceil N/n \rceil$ objects.

<Callout type="key">
The entire game is **choosing the boxes cleverly.** The objects are usually given; your creativity goes into defining categories (remainders, pairs, regions, sums…) so that "two in one box" means exactly what you want to prove.
</Callout>

## Classic examples

**1. Same remainder.** Among any 13 integers, two leave the same remainder mod 12 (boxes = 12 remainders). Their difference is divisible by 12.

**2. Friends at a party.** In any group of $n \ge 2$ people, two have the same number of friends within the group. (Boxes = possible friend-counts $0..n-1$, but $0$ and $n-1$ can't both occur — so really $n-1$ boxes for $n$ people.)

**3. Points in a square.** Place 5 points in a unit square; two are within `√2⁄2 ≈ 0.707` of each other. (Divide the square into 4 smaller squares — 5 points, 4 boxes.)

<Callout type="tip">
ISI/CMI red flag: whenever a problem says "show that **there exist** two … with some shared property," try pigeonhole first. The phrase *there exist two* is practically a signal.
</Callout>

## A worked ISI-style problem

*Prove that among any 6 people, either 3 mutually know each other or 3 mutually don't.*

Pick one person $A$. They have 5 relationships (know / don't know) — by pigeonhole, at least 3 are the same type, say $A$ knows $B, C, D$. If any two of $B,C,D$ know each other, that pair plus $A$ form a "mutual-knowing" triple. If none do, then $B,C,D$ are a "mutual-stranger" triple. Either way, done. (This is the famous $R(3,3)=6$ Ramsey result.)

Pigeonhole rewards a particular kind of cleverness: inventing the right boxes. Do 15–20 varied problems and your eye for "objects vs boxes" becomes instinctive — exactly the muscle ISI/CMI tests.
